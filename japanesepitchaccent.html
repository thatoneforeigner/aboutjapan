What is Pitch Accent?

Pitch Accent is described best by the difference in rising or lowered pitch when pronouncing a word in Japanese.
4 different types of pitch accent
Every word in Japanese has a type of pitch accent that creates a particular sound for its word.
It is fundamental in Japanese intonation and is key for a non-native speaker to understand if they are trying to acquire the language to reach native-fluency.

Why is Pitch Accent Important?

In the Japanese language, there are only 46 basic kana (what we would call letters) in each of the two alphabets (hiragana and katakana).
Furthermore, these kana are completely phonetic which means that their sound does NOT change depending on what word it is.
This is important when it comes to Japanese because when it comes Kanji, its third writing system, there tend to be many homophones in the thousands of kanji present.
Kanji is derived from the Chinese writing system hence the name 漢字 (kanji) [Chinese Letters]. These Chinese letters have tons of similarly spelled words but in China are pronounced differently due to a part of intonation they have known as "Tones." This partly means that when being transferred into the Japanese writing system, there were tons of similarly spelled and pronounced words.
This thankfully has changed over time making the language much easier to understand. I believe pitch accent has a good amount to do with this change because without it, certain words become very ambiguous and confusing and can mean two or more totally different things from each other.

Is Pitch Accent Necessary?

Like mentioned earlier, pitch accent is one of the core parts to Japanese. It differentiates meanings between words with the same spelling and is the basis to natural sounding japanese intonation.
However, for most instances, pitch accent is not entirely necessary.
In most daily conversations, while you're pitch accent may be off, most native speakers can understand what you're trying to say by context. Instead, it's more important to focus on speaking gramatically correct rather than trying to perfect the Japanese pronunciation. There are definitely many cases (and professions) where you do NOT need perfect pitch accent as far as the language is concerned.

Learning Pitch Accent

That being said, if you want to pursue pitch accent, here is an important tip to consider:
Don't focus on the process of learning how to analyze pitch accent, but instead be able to listen to the overall differences in pitch and subconsciously conform your speech into that way. At first, it seems daunting with all those pitch accent arrows and thousands of words to study pitch accent for, however the goal is not to learn all of it, but instead put a subconscious base into your mind that allows you to process accented words natively and intuitively without thinking about it (It's much faster to process things in a language without translating first from your primary language).
Just like how you learn and memorize your times table and PEMDAS for elementary math so you don't even have to think about it when doing harder math classes (i.e. derivatives for calculus), pitch accent operates the same way. It's unrealistic to think you can master every single pitch accent in Japanese without having the prerequisite understanding already engrained in your brain. However, it's unrealistic as well to ignore and toss aside the existence of a fundamental part of a language and think you've attained native-fluency. Foreign language acquisition is different from innate in that being a native speaker doesn't require you to think or even acknowledge these discrepancies, but a foreign language will.

Personal Thoughts On Pitch Accent

I find that many people particularly argue back and forth between the practicality of learning pitch accent: whether it's useful for learners or if it's too much of a hassle.
I personally think that there is no right side on the matter as pursuing 100% proficiency in any language is a personal choice and has its own set of pros and cons.
Many people are completely fine with just being fluent in a language and being able to have proper, natural conversations without the extra unnecessary semantics that not even native speakers know. Plus in real life, there aren't many cases where people will berate your accent nor likely care for that matter. The differences between in how you say "箸「はし」 (HAshi)" or "橋「はし」 (haSHI)" ultimately DO NOT MATTER as long as there is CONTEXT for someone to understand.
However, if you aspire to be as good sounding in Japanese as you can get, then there is no fault in learning and mastering pitch accent either.
In the end, It's just up to what YOU want to get out of the language.
